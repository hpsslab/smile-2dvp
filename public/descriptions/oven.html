Laboratory Oven

Process Description: A laboratory oven is an insulated chamber used to provide a stable, high-temperature environment for various processes. In a cleanroom setting, its primary use is for the hard-baking of photoresists after development to improve their chemical and physical resistance for subsequent steps like etching. Other applications include curing polymers (like SU-8 or polyimide), dehydrating substrates to promote adhesion, and annealing materials. The forced air circulation within the oven ensures a uniform and stable temperature throughout the chamber. Its programmable controller allows for precise temperature ramping and dwelling profiles, which are essential for reproducible and optimized curing and annealing recipes.

System Components:

  • Heating Chamber: An insulated inner chamber, made of stainless steel, where the samples are placed.
  • Controller: The electronic interface with a digital display and keypad. It allows the user to program the setpoint temperature and a timer function for timed baking processes.
  • Door: An insulated door with a secure latch to maintain the internal temperature and prevent heat loss.

Operation Procedure:

  1. Turn on the main power switch for the oven.
  2. Using the controller, set the desired temperature for your process.
  3. If your process is timed, set the desired duration on the timer.
  4. Wait for the oven to preheat and stabilize at the setpoint temperature. The digital display will indicate the current internal temperature.
  5. Once stable, open the door and, using high-temperature-rated tweezers, carefully place your sample onto a shelf inside the chamber.
  6. Close the oven door securely. Start the timer if it doesn't start automatically.
  7. After the bake is complete, turn off the oven or allow it to cool down.
  8. Using tweezers, carefully remove the hot sample and place it on a heat-resistant surface to cool.

Safety Warnings:

  • All interior and exterior surfaces of the oven can become extremely hot and can cause severe burns.
  • Always use appropriate high-temperature tweezers or tongs to handle samples and items inside the oven.
  • Open the door slowly and stand to the side to avoid a blast of hot air.
  • Only place materials in the oven that are rated for the set temperature. Do not place flammable solvents or low-melting-point plastics inside.
  • Ensure the over-temperature protection is set to a reasonable value just above your process temperature.
