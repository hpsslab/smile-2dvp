Electron Beam Evaporator

Process Description: An electron beam evaporator is a physical vapor deposition system used to deposit high-purity thin films of metals and dielectric materials onto a substrate. Inside a high-vacuum chamber, a high-energy electron beam is generated and magnetically steered to strike a source material (e.g., gold, titanium, aluminum oxide) held in a crucible. The intense energy from the electron beam heats the material, causing it to sublimate or evaporate. This vapor then travels in a line-of-sight path and condenses on the cooler substrates mounted at the top of the chamber, forming a thin film. This technique is favored for its ability to deposit high-melting-point materials and create very pure films. The deposition rate and final film thickness are precisely monitored in real-time, often using a quartz crystal microbalance, allowing for exceptional control over the film's properties.

System Components:

  • Deposition Chamber: The main high-vacuum chamber where the deposition process occurs. It houses the electron beam gun, crucible turret, substrate holder, and thickness monitor.
  • Observation Window: A shielded viewport that allows the operator to safely observe the electron beam hitting the source material as well as the sample and monitor the evaporation process.

Operation Procedure:

  1. Log in to the control system and verify the chamber is under high vacuum.
  2. Vent the load-lock and carefully mount the substrate(s) onto the sample holder.
  3. Place the holder in the load-lock, close the door, and pump it down.
  4. Once at vacuum, transfer the sample holder into the main deposition chamber.
  5. Using the control software, select the desired source material and load the deposition recipe.
  6. Start the process, which involves ramping up the high voltage and electron beam current.
  7. Once the material is molten and the deposition rate is stable, a shutter opens, and the deposition on the substrate begins.
  8. Monitor the film thickness in real-time using the quartz crystal monitor.
  9. When the target thickness is reached, the shutter closes, and the beam power is ramped down.
  10. After a cool-down period, transfer the samples back to the load-lock, vent it, and retrieve your coated substrates.

Safety Warnings:

  • The electron gun operates at extremely high voltages (10kV or more) and generates intense, harmful X-rays during operation. The chamber is heavily shielded with lead. Never defeat any safety interlocks.
  • Never attempt to open the main chamber door while it is under vacuum. Always use the load-lock.
  • The crucible and source materials become extremely hot and can cause severe burns if handled improperly after a run.
  • If the system uses a cryopump, be aware of procedures for handling liquid nitrogen or compressed helium.
