Laboratory Hot Plate

Process Description: A laboratory hot plate is a fundamental piece of equipment used to provide precise and uniform heating to samples. In a cleanroom or chemistry lab setting, its primary applications include soft-baking and hard-baking of photoresists, post-exposure baking, and evaporating solvents from substrates. The flat, heated surface ensures consistent thermal transfer, which is critical for achieving reproducible results in lithography and thin-film processing. Many models also include a magnetic stirring function for heating liquids in beakers. Digital controls on modern hot plates allow for the precise setting and monitoring of temperature to ensure process accuracy and repeatability.

System Components:

  • Heating Surface: A flat top plate, made of ceramic or aluminum, that provides a uniform temperature distribution for the sample.
  • Temperature Controller: An electronic module with a digital display that allows the user to set and monitor the surface temperature. It uses feedback from an internal sensor to maintain the set point.
  • Control Knob/Buttons: Interface for setting the desired temperature and, if equipped, the magnetic stirring speed.

Operation Procedure:

  1. Ensure the hot plate is on a level, stable surface, and if using solvents, inside a certified fume hood.
  2. Turn on the power switch.
  3. Using the control knob or buttons, set the desired temperature for your process.
  4. Wait for the hot plate to reach and stabilize at the set temperature. The display will indicate when the actual temperature matches the set point.
  5. Using appropriate tweezers, carefully place your sample or wafer in the center of the heating surface.
  6. Time the baking/heating process according to your recipe.
  7. Once the time is complete, carefully remove the sample using tweezers and place it on a cooling rack.
  8. Turn the heat setting to zero or off and allow the hot plate to cool down completely before cleaning or storing.

Safety Warnings:

  • The surface can reach very high temperatures and remains hot long after being turned off. It can cause severe burns. Do not touch the surface.
  • Always handle samples with heat-resistant tweezers.
  • When heating any solvents or chemicals, the hot plate must be used inside a properly functioning chemical fume hood.
  • Do not leave a hot plate unattended while it is in operation.
  • Avoid placing cold glassware directly onto a very hot surface to prevent thermal shock and shattering.
